Digital Well-being Vision and Practice

Lesson 4 Learning Objectives

After this lesson, participants will be able to:

Create a personal vision for digital well-being that aligns with their values and goals

Understand the importance of social support and accountability in maintaining digital well-being

Sustainable Practices

Develop sustainable practices and routines that support long-term digital well-being

Progress Tracking

Learn strategies for tracking progress and making adjustments to their digital well-being journey

Building a Positive Relationship with Technology

Key Point

Building a positive relationship with technology requires intentional design of your digital environment, regular self-reflection, and ongoing adjustments that align with your evolving values and life circumstances.

A healthy relationship with technology isn't about perfection—it's about awareness and intentionality.

Key strategies include:

  • Design your environment: Curate your digital spaces to support your well-being goals.
  • Practice regular reflection: Check in with yourself about how technology is serving you.
  • Stay flexible: Adjust your approach as your needs and circumstances change.
  • Focus on progress, not perfection: Celebrate small wins and learn from setbacks.

Staying Accountable

Key Point

Sustainable digital well-being thrives with accountability systems that include regular self-check-ins, trusted support partners, and structured reflection practices that help you stay aligned with your goals and values over time.

Accountability isn't about judgment—it's about support and consistency.

Effective accountability strategies:

  • Find an accountability partner: Share your goals with someone who will support your journey.
  • Schedule regular check-ins: Set aside time weekly or monthly to reflect on your progress.
  • Track your habits: Use simple tools to monitor your digital behaviors and patterns.
  • Celebrate progress: Acknowledge your successes, no matter how small.
  • Adjust when needed: Be willing to modify your approach based on what you learn.

Reviewing Your Digital Goals

Key Point

Regular goal review is essential for maintaining momentum in your digital well-being journey. Celebrate your progress, learn from challenges, and adjust your goals to reflect your evolving needs and circumstances.

Goal review isn't about perfection—it's about learning and growth.

When reviewing your goals:

  • Acknowledge progress: Recognize what you've accomplished, even small steps.
  • Identify challenges: What obstacles have you encountered? What can you learn from them?
  • Assess relevance: Do your goals still align with your current values and priorities?
  • Make adjustments: Modify your goals based on what you've learned about yourself.
  • Set new intentions: What do you want to focus on moving forward?

Creating New Routines for Digital Flourishing

Key Point

Digital flourishing happens when you intentionally design daily routines that support your well-being goals, starting small with sustainable changes that gradually become natural parts of your life.

Sustainable routines are built on consistency, not perfection.

Keys to successful routine building:

  • Start small: Begin with one or two simple changes you can maintain consistently.
  • Link to existing habits: Attach new digital habits to routines you already have.
  • Design your environment: Make healthy choices easier and unhealthy ones harder.
  • Be patient: Allow time for new routines to become automatic.
  • Stay flexible: Adjust your routines as your life circumstances change.

Rituals for Flourishing

Key Point

Meaningful rituals create intentional transitions and moments of presence in your day, helping you maintain balance and connection with what matters most, even in a digitally connected world.

Rituals are different from habits—they carry meaning and intention.

Powerful digital well-being rituals:

  • Morning intention setting: Begin your day with purpose before checking devices.
  • Transition rituals: Create boundaries between work and personal time.
  • Evening wind-down: Establish tech-free time before sleep.
  • Weekly digital sabbath: Set aside regular time for offline activities.
  • Mindful check-ins: Pause regularly to assess how technology is serving you.

Reflection Question

Take a moment to reflect and write your response to the question below.

Sleep and Digital Health

Key Point

Quality sleep is fundamental to digital well-being. Protecting your sleep from digital disruption through evening routines, bedroom boundaries, and mindful device use supports both physical health and emotional resilience.

Sleep and technology have a complex relationship that requires intentional management.

Essential sleep and digital health practices:

  • Create a digital sunset: Stop using screens 1-2 hours before bedtime.
  • Keep devices out of the bedroom: Use an analog alarm clock instead of your phone.
  • Use blue light filters: Reduce screen brightness and blue light in the evening.
  • Establish a wind-down routine: Replace screen time with calming activities.
  • Be mindful of content: Avoid stimulating or stressful content before sleep.

Reflection Question

Take a moment to reflect and write your response to the question below.

Glossary of Digital Well-being Concepts

A B C D E F H M N P R S T W

A

Accountability Buddy – A supportive partner who helps track progress and encourages consistency in digital well-being goals.
Attention Management – The practice of consciously directing focus in a digital environment to enhance productivity and cognitive health.
Autonomy (Self-Determination Theory) – The ability to make choices about digital engagement that align with personal values and goals.

B

Brain Cognition – The way the brain processes information, impacted by digital habits such as screen time and multitasking.
Behavioral Science – The study of how people form digital habits and how technology influences decision-making.

C

Character Strengths – Personal strengths (e.g., curiosity, self-regulation) that can be leveraged to cultivate healthy digital habits.
Cognitive Load – The mental effort required to process information, which can be impacted by excessive screen time or multitasking.
Core Values – Fundamental beliefs that guide digital habits and decision-making.

D

Digital Detox – A period of time spent without digital devices to reset habits and improve well-being.
Digital Flourishing – Thriving in a digital environment by balancing technology use with personal and professional well-being.
Digital Goals – Personalized objectives for improving one's digital habits, often using the SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) framework.
Digital Habit Audit – A self-assessment to track technology use and identify areas for improvement.
Digital Minimalism – A philosophy advocating for intentional and purposeful use of technology to enhance well-being.

E

Empathy and Technology – The relationship between digital interactions and emotional intelligence, including how technology can impact social connections.
Energy Management – Strategies to prevent digital fatigue and optimize energy levels in a tech-heavy world.

F

Focus & Productivity – The ability to maintain attention and effectiveness in work and life, often challenged by digital distractions.
Flourishing (Well-being Theory) – The state of thriving in multiple aspects of life, including physical, mental, emotional, and digital well-being.

H

Habit Loop – A framework for understanding habit formation, consisting of cue, routine, and reward, applicable to digital behaviors.

M

Mindfulness in Technology Use – Practicing present-moment awareness to create intentional and balanced digital interactions.
Motivation (Self-Determination Theory) – Internal and external factors that influence engagement with technology, including autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

N

Notification Management – Strategies to reduce interruptions and increase focus, such as disabling non-essential alerts.

P

Prefrontal Cortex – The part of the brain responsible for decision-making and impulse control, which is affected by digital engagement.
Psychological Safety and Technology – The impact of digital environments on emotional well-being and security.

R

Reflection Practice – Periodic self-check-ins to assess digital habits and their alignment with personal values.

S

Screen Time Awareness – Conscious tracking of time spent on digital devices to maintain a balanced lifestyle.
Self-Regulation – The ability to control impulses and make mindful choices in digital interactions.
Social Media Hygiene – Practices for maintaining a healthy relationship with social platforms, including curating content and setting boundaries.

T

Tech-Life Balance – The intentional effort to harmonize technology use with overall well-being.
Triggers in Digital Use – Environmental or psychological cues that prompt certain digital habits, such as checking the phone out of boredom.
Two-Edge Effect of Technology – The concept that digital tools can both enhance and hinder well-being, depending on usage patterns.

W

Willpower and Digital Use – The ability to resist distractions and build positive technology habits.
