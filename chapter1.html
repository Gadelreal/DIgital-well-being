LESSON 1: Digital Well-being as Key to Human Flourishing

Lesson 1 Learning Objectives

After this lesson, participants will be able to:

Define Digital Well-being

Define digital well-being and explain its significance

Holistic Understanding

Develop a holistic understanding of how technology impacts the body, mind, and soul—considering the roles of awareness, science, and practice in the learning journey

Personal Motivations

Identify personal motivations and set intentions to actively cultivate digital well-being

Purpose and Values

Reflect on purpose and values, and recognize how digital behaviors can align with or detract from them

The Wake-Up Call

What Is Digital Well-being and What Is Not?

Key Point

Digital well-being is about building self-awareness and intentionally shaping habits around technology to support our emotional, mental, and physical health—based on our individual values and what helps us be our best.

Activity: What Is Digital Well-being and What Is Not?

Select all the correct answers that reflect what digital well-being is truly about.

Digital well-being question

1. Digital well-being is about...

Digital well-being is not about completely rejecting technology or feeling guilty about using it. Rather, it's about being mindful of how we engage with digital spaces.

The correct answers are:

  • a. ... a set of practices and habits that you can cultivate every day.
  • e. ... how we feel about the time we spend online.

These responses reflect the idea that digital well-being is an ongoing process of balance and self-awareness - not a fixed destination or extreme action.

Keep considering how your digital habits impact your well-being!

What Role Does Technology Play in Your Life?

Explore how technology influences different aspects of our daily lives and well-being.

Raising Awareness: The Impact of Technology on Well-being

Key Point

Raising awareness of our daily digital habits—especially around sleep, focus, and relationships—helps us understand their impact on our holistic well-being and empowers us to make intentional adjustments that support body, mind, and soul.

As we transition into exploring digital habits more deeply, take a moment to reflect on your current behaviors. Are your devices helping or hindering your sleep, your ability to focus, your sense of calm, or your relationships? This awareness is the first step toward meaningful and sustainable change.

What Motivates You to Cultivate Your Digital Well-being?

Key Point

Lasting change in digital well-being starts with intrinsic motivation—grounded in autonomy, competence, and relatedness—and requires a proactive, personalized approach that aligns with your values and purpose.

True behavioral change begins when we understand why we want to change. Reflect on what really matters to you and how your digital habits can support—not hinder—your values and goals.

Motivation is stronger and more sustainable when it's rooted in autonomy (your ability to choose), competence (your belief in your ability to grow), and relatedness (your sense of connection with others).

Reflection Question

Take a moment to reflect and write your response to the question below.

Based on the principles of Self-Determination Theory—autonomy, competence, and relatedness—can you identify three factors that motivate you to maintain or pursue digital well-being?

Aligning Your Values with Digital Habits

Key Point

Clarifying your core values is essential to making intentional decisions about your digital habits, aligning technology use with what truly matters to you strengthens well-being, focus, and authentic living.

Our digital habits are most sustainable and meaningful when they reflect our deepest values. By identifying what truly matters—whether it's integrity, creativity, connection, or balance—you can design digital behaviors that support your personal and professional well-being.

Take this opportunity to explore your core values and let them guide your intentional use of technology.

Setting Digital Goals

Key Point

Effective digital goals are SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), values-based, and focused on building positive habits rather than just restricting negative ones.

Setting clear, actionable goals helps transform your awareness and values into concrete steps toward digital well-being. Remember to start small, be specific, and focus on what you want to gain from your digital habits, not just what you want to avoid.

Activity: Setting Your Digital Well-being Goals

Now that you've identified your core values and reflected on your digital habits, it's time to set specific, actionable goals for your digital well-being journey.

Instructions:

  1. Download the Digital Goals Template below
  2. Use the SMART framework to create 2-3 specific digital well-being goals
  3. Ensure your goals align with the core values you identified earlier
  4. Include both short-term (1-2 weeks) and medium-term (1-2 months) goals
  5. Focus on positive habits you want to build, not just restrictions

This template includes the SMART framework, examples of digital well-being goals, and space for tracking your progress.

Lesson 1 Summary

Holistic Impact: Technology affects our body, mind, and soul through sleep patterns, focus, relationships, and overall life satisfaction.

Self-Determination Theory: Sustainable motivation comes from autonomy (choice), competence (growth), and relatedness (connection).

Values Alignment: Core values serve as a compass for making intentional decisions about digital habits.

SMART Goals Framework: Learned to create Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound goals for digital well-being.

Reflection Practice: Engaged in multiple reflection exercises to build self-awareness about current digital habits and motivations for change.

Awareness Building: Developed understanding of how technology impacts sleep, focus, and relationships.

Continue Reflection: Keep observing your digital habits throughout the week and notice how they align with your identified values.

Implement Small Changes: Begin working on the digital goals you set, starting with small, manageable steps.

Stay Curious: Maintain an attitude of curiosity and self-compassion as you explore your relationship with technology.

Glossary of Digital Well-being Concepts

A B C D E F H M N P R S T W

A

Accountability Buddy – A supportive partner who helps track progress and encourages consistency in digital well-being goals.
Attention Management – The practice of consciously directing focus in a digital environment to enhance productivity and cognitive health.
Autonomy (Self-Determination Theory) – The ability to make choices about digital engagement that align with personal values and goals.

B

Brain Cognition – The way the brain processes information, impacted by digital habits such as screen time and multitasking.
Behavioral Science – The study of how people form digital habits and how technology influences decision-making.

C

Character Strengths – Personal strengths (e.g., curiosity, self-regulation) that can be leveraged to cultivate healthy digital habits.
Cognitive Load – The mental effort required to process information, which can be impacted by excessive screen time or multitasking.
Core Values – Fundamental beliefs that guide digital habits and decision-making.

D

Digital Detox – A period of time spent without digital devices to reset habits and improve well-being.
Digital Flourishing – Thriving in a digital environment by balancing technology use with personal and professional well-being.
Digital Goals – Personalized objectives for improving one's digital habits, often using the SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) framework.
Digital Habit Audit – A self-assessment to track technology use and identify areas for improvement.
Digital Minimalism – A philosophy advocating for intentional and purposeful use of technology to enhance well-being.

E

Empathy and Technology – The relationship between digital interactions and emotional intelligence, including how technology can impact social connections.
Energy Management – Strategies to prevent digital fatigue and optimize energy levels in a tech-heavy world.

F

Focus & Productivity – The ability to maintain attention and effectiveness in work and life, often challenged by digital distractions.
Flourishing (Well-being Theory) – The state of thriving in multiple aspects of life, including physical, mental, emotional, and digital well-being.

H

Habit Loop – A framework for understanding habit formation, consisting of cue, routine, and reward, applicable to digital behaviors.

M

Mindfulness in Technology Use – Practicing present-moment awareness to create intentional and balanced digital interactions.
Motivation (Self-Determination Theory) – Internal and external factors that influence engagement with technology, including autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

N

Notification Management – Strategies to reduce interruptions and increase focus, such as disabling non-essential alerts.

P

Prefrontal Cortex – The part of the brain responsible for decision-making and impulse control, which is affected by digital engagement.
Psychological Safety and Technology – The impact of digital environments on emotional well-being and security.

R

Reflection Practice – Periodic self-check-ins to assess digital habits and their alignment with personal values.

S

Screen Time Awareness – Conscious tracking of time spent on digital devices to maintain a balanced lifestyle.
Self-Regulation – The ability to control impulses and make mindful choices in digital interactions.
Social Media Hygiene – Practices for maintaining a healthy relationship with social platforms, including curating content and setting boundaries.

T

Tech-Life Balance – The intentional effort to harmonize technology use with overall well-being.
Triggers in Digital Use – Environmental or psychological cues that prompt certain digital habits, such as checking the phone out of boredom.
Two-Edge Effect of Technology – The concept that digital tools can both enhance and hinder well-being, depending on usage patterns.

W

Willpower and Digital Use – The ability to resist distractions and build positive technology habits.
